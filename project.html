The Failure of the Skill India Mission and Building a Future-Ready Skilling Framework

Aims & Research Questions

Project Overview

India's demographic dividendâ€”with 60% working-age population and median age under 30â€”creates unprecedented opportunity for growth. Yet 25% of youth are NEET, only 4.7% have formal vocational training, and youth unemployment reaches 30%. The Skill India Mission (SIM) trained 160 million but placement rates collapsed from 18.4% to 10.1%. This project examines why SIM failed and how curriculum alignment with domestic and international labor demand can transform outcomes.

Research Questions: (1) Why has PMKVY failed despite training millions? (2) How can curriculum alignment improve employability? (3) What reforms could deliver quality skills?

Conclusions

What the Evidence Suggests

The quantitative analysis reveals three critical findings. First, PMKVY's failures transcend state-level capacity differencesâ€”even Gujarat experienced catastrophic placement collapse, suggesting systematic program design flaws. Second, government de-linked placement tracking from PMKVY 4.0 (FY 2022-23), eliminating accountability while claiming success based solely on training volume.

Third, India's demographic dividend creates a 20-30 year window that is narrowing as youth dependency declines and elderly dependency rises. Combined with global labor shortages totaling 50 million workers by 2030, India could expand annual migration from 700K to 2.5M workersâ€”but only through fundamental SIM reform focused on curriculum-industry alignment, international certification standards, and quality-over-quantity metrics.

Data Sources & Methodology

Data, Tools & Challenges

Data Sources

  • PMKVY Data: PIB Press Release PRID 2204132 (Dec 2025) - State-wise training and placement FY 2020-25
  • Demographics: World Bank WDI, UN Population Division (1990-2024)
  • Labor Market: PLFS Annual Reports, India Graduate Skills Index 2025
  • Migration: GATI Report 2025, OECD Labor Statistics, Ministry of External Affairs
  • State Economics: RBI State Finance Reports, Census 2011

Data Challenges

  • Placement De-linking: Government stopped tracking placement from FY 2022-23, eliminating accountability for employment outcomes
  • Administrative Variations: State name inconsistencies required manual matching across datasets
  • Missing Granularity: Sector-wise placement data not published, preventing industry-specific analysis
  • Projection Uncertainty: Global shortage estimates vary across sources; used conservative GATI figures

Analytical Tools

  • Python 3.x: pandas for data cleaning, merging PIB annexures
  • scikit-learn: LinearRegression for state-level predictors, StandardScaler for normalization
  • Statistical Methods: Difference-in-Differences (Gujarat vs Manipur)
  • Visualization: Vega-Lite 5.x with interactive parameters (sliders, selectors, hover effects)
  • Reproducibility: Google Colab notebooks with public access

Research Design

Comparative Case Study: Gujarat (advanced industrial) vs Manipur (peripheral agrarian) to test whether state capacity moderates program outcomes. Theoretical Framework: Principal-agent theory for policy implementation, CIPP model for evaluation. Analysis: Triangulation of official data with qualitative stakeholder interviews and international comparisons (Germany's Indo-German Green Skills programme).
