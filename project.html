Skill India Mission: Examining Skilling, Workforce Mobility and Sectoral Employment Outcomes in India

Aims

Aim and Objective of the Project

India is in the midst of a demographic dividend, with roughly 60% of its population in the working‑age bracket (15–59) and a median age under 30. This demographic bulge presents us a window of opportunity to fuel rapid growth, but only if the young population are productively engaged. Yet nearly a quarter of the youth population is not in education, employment or training (NEET) and only about 4.7% of the workforce has formal vocational training. According to the India Graduate Skills Index 2025, youth unemployment hovers near 30%, with many graduates struggling to find jobs.

The Skill India Mission (SIM) and its flagship Pradhan Mantri Kaushal Vikas Yojana (PMKVY) were launched to address this gap by training millions of young people. As of December 2024, SIM claimed to have trained more than 160 million individuals. However, the outcomes remain poor. PMKVY's placement rate has stagnated at 18.4% under its first phase, 23.4% in the second, and just 10.1% in the third. Understanding why SIM and PMKVY are failing and identifying reforms that link training to industry needs and international mobility are therefore urgent policy priorities.

Data

Sources and Access

This analysis draws on multiple authoritative data sources to examine India's skill development landscape comprehensively. Primary quantitative data comes from the Ministry of Skill Development and Entrepreneurship (MSDE) annual reports and Press Information Bureau releases, which provide state-wise PMKVY training and placement figures from FY 2015-16 to FY 2024-25. Labor market indicators including youth unemployment rates, NEET statistics, and workforce participation are sourced from the Ministry of Statistics' Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) annual reports.

Demographic data comes from the World Bank World Development Indicators and UN Population Division, providing age dependency ratios and working-age population shares for comparative analysis. International migration and global labor demand projections are compiled from ILO databases, bilateral Migration and Mobility Partnership agreements, and OECD labor market statistics. State-level economic indicators including GDP per capita, industrial output shares, and literacy rates are obtained from Reserve Bank of India state finance reports and Census 2011 data.

Tools

Cleaning and Analysis

Data processing and analysis employed Python 3.x with pandas for data manipulation, particularly for cleaning parliamentary question annexures and merging datasets across different administrative classifications. The scikit-learn library enabled supervised learning regression to identify predictors of placement success, with StandardScaler for feature normalization and LinearRegression for coefficient estimation. Difference-in-Differences analysis compared Gujarat (advanced state) versus Manipur (peripheral state) to test whether state capacity moderates program outcomes.

Visualization development used Vega-Lite 5.x for interactive charts with parameter-based filtering, enabling year sliders, metric selectors, and hover effects for user exploration. All analysis was conducted in Google Colab notebooks for reproducibility, with code and data publicly accessible. Charts were embedded in a responsive HTML/CSS portfolio using vegaEmbed, with consistent styling through Poppins font family. The analytical pipeline demonstrates integration of multiple techniques: API data retrieval, web scraping, statistical modeling, and interactive visualization design.

Conclusions

What the Evidence Suggests

The quantitative analysis reveals three critical findings. First, PMKVY's failures transcend state-level capacity differences—even Gujarat, an advanced industrial state, experienced catastrophic placement rate collapse from 22.5% to 5.9% between FY 2020-21 and 2021-22, suggesting systematic program design flaws rather than implementation issues. Second, the government's decision to de-link placement tracking from PMKVY 4.0 onwards (from FY 2022-23) eliminates accountability for the program's core employment outcome, representing metric manipulation to obscure failure.

Third, machine learning analysis across states demonstrates that literacy rates and existing formal skill training infrastructure are significantly stronger predictors of placement success than state GDP per capita, challenging infrastructure-heavy policy approaches and supporting curriculum reform recommendations. Combined with India's narrowing demographic dividend window (working-age population will peak and decline as elderly dependency rises), these findings underscore the urgency of fundamental PMKVY redesign focused on curriculum-industry alignment, quality over quantity metrics, and integration with international labor mobility pathways.
